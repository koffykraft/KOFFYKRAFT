Lesson 3.1: Cupping in Real-Life Conditions (Revised)

Professional cupping rooms are clean, quiet, well-lit, and designed to remove distraction. But your real-life tasting space may be your kitchen, your veranda, or a corner of your shop. This lesson helps you cup in any environment — calmly, mindfully, and honestly — by understanding how your surroundings affect your perception.

Objectives

- Learn how light, sound, time, and mood impact your sensory clarity

- Begin identifying your best tasting conditions

- Build skill in filtering out noise and grounding yourself in the cup

- Reduce anxiety about ‘ideal setups’ and make the most of what you have

Tools Needed

- 2 cups of the same coffee (prepared identically)

- A notebook or printed log

- Access to two different settings (e.g. bright room vs. low light, quiet vs. active)

- Optional: thermometer, stopwatch, or light meter (not required)

Protocol – Real-World Cupping Practice

1. Prepare 2 cups of coffee using the same grind, water temperature, and time.

2. Taste one cup in your usual cupping spot. Note lighting, noise, temperature, mood.

3. Taste the second cup in a different spot or time of day. Note the same variables.

4. Focus on basic observations: Did sweetness, body, or clarity change?

5. Don’t worry about vocabulary. Use words like ‘clearer’, ‘muddier’, ‘brighter’.

6. Record how you felt during each session — anxious, focused, relaxed, distracted.

7. Repeat the next day and see if your preferences stay consistent.

Self-Check

- Which environment helped you taste more clearly?

- Did your mood affect your ability to describe what you tasted?

- Are you becoming more aware of *when* and *where* you taste best?

Before You Move On

You don’t need the perfect lab to learn cupping. You need awareness and honesty. Repeat this lesson until you feel confident tasting even in slightly imperfect conditions. This skill will serve you in competitions, cafés, or the field.

Beginner caution

Cupping is a noticing practice, not an exam. Scores and flavor words should support honest observation, not pressure to sound professional.

Before you continue

  • Write the main idea in one sentence.
  • Try one small exercise or observation from the lesson.
  • Record what you tasted, smelled, changed, or noticed without judging yourself.

Lesson 1.1: Aroma Mastery

The learning loop

  1. Notice: smell or taste slowly before naming.
  2. Name: write simple words first; refine later.
  3. Compare: check another cup, stage, or reference.
  4. Record: write what changed and what stayed stable.
  5. Repeat: make one small improvement next session.
What you are learning

Aroma is the first signal your brain receives from coffee. In cupping you observe aroma in stages: dry grounds, wet crust, and the moment you break the crust.

Why it matters

Beginners often jump straight to flavor words. Aroma practice slows you down and teaches you to separate what the nose notices before the tongue gets involved.

Beginner translation

If a dry coffee smells like cocoa but the wet crust smells like ripe fruit, do not force one word to cover both moments. Write both. The change itself is useful information.

From the KoffyKraft notes

This is your first sensory lesson in the KoffyKraft Cupping Series. Aroma is often the first and strongest impression we get from a cup of coffee. Here, you will learn to identify and describe aroma in three stages: dry, wet, and after the crust break.

Objectives

  • Learn the importance of aroma in cupping
  • Understand the difference between dry and wet aroma
  • Train your nose to detect broad aroma categories
  • Build vocabulary using real references

Tools Needed

  • 3 coffee samples (same roast, different origins recommended)
  • Hot water at 93 deg C
  • Grinder
  • Cupping bowls or similar-sized glasses (150ml x 3)
  • Timer
  • Tasting form or notebook

Cupping Protocol - Aroma Focus Only

  1. Grind 8.25g of coffee per cup.
  2. Smell the dry grounds. Note what you sense (fruity, nutty, earthy, etc.).
  3. Pour 150 ml water at 93 deg C into each cup.
  4. Wait 4 minutes.
  5. Smell the wet aroma (sniff the surface).
  6. Break the crust with a spoon. Smell immediately.
  7. Write down the dominant aromas at each stage.

Self-Check

  • Could you identify at least one aroma in all three stages?
  • Were you consistent in describing the same cup?
  • How confident are you that these descriptions were repeatable?

Before You Move On

Repeat this lesson on three different coffees across three different sessions. Only when you can consistently describe aromas confidently in all three stages should you move to Lesson 1.2.

Practice this way

  1. Prepare the cups as described in the original notes.
  2. Before tasting, write the question for this session in one sentence.
  3. Taste in stages: hot, warm, and cooler. Do not rush to a final answer.
  4. Use plain language first. Add professional terms only when they help.
  5. Review your notes after ten minutes and underline what feels repeatable.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trying to sound professional instead of writing what you actually smell.
  • Smelling too hard until the nose gets tired.
  • Using one aroma word for all stages.

Self-check with answers

1. What is the main skill in this lesson?

Answer: Dry aroma is before water; wet aroma is after water contacts the grounds; crust-break aroma is the burst released when the crust is opened.

2. What should you do if your note feels uncertain?

Answer: A broad word such as fruity, nutty, floral, earthy, spicy, or roasty is enough for a beginner.

3. What makes the observation more reliable?

Answer: If you cannot name an aroma, describe a memory or object it reminds you of.

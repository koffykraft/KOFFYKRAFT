Module Glossary

Canonical definitions for recurring roasting terms. Lesson definitions may be shorter; this page holds the more careful wording.

Agtron Scale

A standardized roast-color measurement system. Useful, but still interpreted alongside flavor, bean surface, roast age, and machine context.

Baking

A dull or lifeless cup character often associated with insufficient momentum or stalled development. Diagnose it with cup quality, curve behavior, and bean appearance together.

Charge Temperature

The roaster temperature reading at the moment green coffee is loaded. The number depends heavily on machine type, probe position, batch size, and preheat routine.

Development Phase

The practical phase after First Crack where the roaster manages final sweetness, acidity, body, roast depth, and balance. Browning reactions can still continue during this phase.

DTR

Development Time Ratio: the percentage of total roast time after First Crack. Use it as a comparison tool, not as a standalone roast-level definition.

First Crack

The audible popping stage caused by pressure and structural changes inside the bean. It is commonly used as the practical start of Development Phase.

Maillard Phase

The browning phase where amino acids and sugars create many flavor and aroma compounds. It does not stop instantly at First Crack.

Rate of Rise

The speed at which bean temperature is increasing, usually shown as degrees per minute. Treat curve changes as signals to investigate, not automatic proof of a defect.

Sample Roasting

Small-batch roasting designed to reveal green coffee quality consistently and neutrally, rather than maximize a particular beverage style.

Scorching

Surface burning often linked to excessive contact heat or high early energy, but it should be confirmed by bean appearance and cup character.

Solubility

How easily roasted coffee compounds dissolve into brewing water. Darker and more developed coffees are often more soluble, but grind, water, and brew method still matter.

Tipping

Small burnt marks on bean tips or edges. It may come from excessive heat, sensitive beans, airflow, drum speed, or a combination of factors.

Production and Quality / Lesson 3.3

Roast Profiling with Data Tools

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Data tools help only when tied to consistent logging, sensory results, and machine understanding.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds evidence-based profiling.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Learn to track, interpret, and adjust roast curves using software or manual logs to improve repeatability, flavor, and roasting confidence.

A. Why Profiling Matters

  • Enables repeatability of great roasts- Helps identify roast drift or inconsistency- Allows flavor comparison across profiles- Creates a long-term data trail for quality control

B. Roast Curve Components (with Log Parameters)

| Parameter | Description ||-------------------|-------------------------------------------------|| Bean Temp | Measured via thermocouple/probe inside drum || Environment Temp | Hot air around the bean mass || Rate of Rise (RoR)| deg C rise per minute - steepness of bean curve || Turning Point | Lowest temp after charge || Yellowing Time | Approx. 150-160 deg C - end of drying || First Crack | Audible pops ~195-205 deg C || Development Time | From first crack to drop || DTR (%) | Development Time / Total Time x 100 |

C. Ideal Curve Characteristics (Clarified)

| Characteristic | Definition ||----------------------|--------------------------------------------------|| Smooth RoR descent | Gradual drop in RoR until end || No Flick | No RoR spike after First Crack (flick = upturn) || No Crash | No RoR dive after Crack (crash = sharp drop) || Crack between 195-205 deg C | First crack aligns with sugar reaction zone || DTR between 15-25% | Balanced flavor development |

D. Flavor Risks by Profile Shape + Origin

| Curve Feature | Flavor Risk | Origin Context ||--------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|| RoR Spike | Bitter edge | Robusta = risk of harshness if too sharp || RoR Crash | Baked, dull cup | Ethiopia = clarity loss, underdevelopment || Late Crack (>205 deg C)| Sour or dry finish | Yirgacheffe = bright -> sour, if crack is too late || Dev <12% | No sweetness | Most washed origins -> taste grassy or tart || Dev >28% | Muted, roasty | Brazil naturals = lose fruit nuance |

E. Profiling Tools + Troubleshooting

| Tool | Use Case | Tips for Accuracy ||--------------|---------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|| Artisan | Home/Prosumer | Verify RoR spikes, calibrate temp probe regularly || Cropster | Pro/Cloud/Export Labs | Tag cupping scores, sync flavor with curves || RoastLog | Business use, QC teams | Maintain profiles, compare over weeks || Manual Logs | No-software setups | Track turning point, yellowing, crack consistently || Thermometer + Timer| Beginners | Use Excel to plot bean temp over time manually |

F. Exercise - Logging a Profile

Roast **200g of medium-roast Ethiopia Yirgacheffe**, twice.Use same settings. Log:- Time (min:sec)- Bean Temp ( deg C)- RoR (if available)- Notes (crack timing, aroma, drop point)Then cup using V60 1:16. Record:- Acidity- Sweetness- Aftertaste- BodyCompare logs + flavor. Any flicks or crashes?

G. Analogy - Drawing a Roast Road Map

  • The curve = your driving path- Smooth = safe and predictable- Crashes or flicks = overshoot or brake too hard- Goal: repeat the ride with minor adjustments each time

I. Sustainability Note

  • Profiling prevents wasteful re-roasting- Enables consistent QC from smallholder to cafe- Preserves delicate origin flavors and improves value alignment

J. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: 'My log shows a spike just after crack. Flavor was okay but edgy. What should I change?'**Answer**: That's likely a flick. Try reducing gas just before First Crack or slightly increasing post-crack airflow to flatten the RoR.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

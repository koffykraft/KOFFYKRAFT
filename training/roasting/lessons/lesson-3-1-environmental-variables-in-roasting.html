Environmental Variables in Roasting

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Ambient temperature, humidity, green coffee temperature, machine preheat, and ventilation can shift roast behavior.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds seasonal and operational awareness.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Understand how ambient temperature, humidity, and bean conditions influence roast behavior-and how to adapt your approach for consistency.

A. Why Environment Matters

  • External conditions change how heat enters beans.- Poor adaptation = roast drift, inconsistency, or flavor loss.- Great roasters treat environment like another roast variable.

B. Key Environmental Factors (with Origin Context)

| Variable | Effect on Roast | Risk if Ignored | Origin Note ||------------------|------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|| Ambient Temp | Affects bean preheating and RoR lag | Under- or over-development | Yirgacheffe (dense): slower heat uptake when cold || Humidity | Moisture loss, drying rate | Tipping or sourness | Robusta: faster dry in low humidity = scorching risk || Bean Temp | Determines start RoR | Curve distortion, spike or flat spot | Let green beans warm indoors for 2-3 hrs pre-roast || Drum Stability | Thermal momentum for consistent curves | Curve delay or runaway | Use 15-30 min warm-up or dummy roast || Air Pressure | Impacts boiling, crack timing | Confusing crack onset or dev time | High altitude = slower convection |

C. Roast Adjustment by Environmental Condition

See infographic: Environmental Roast Adjustment Chart| Environmental Condition | Adjustment Strategy | Flavor Impact ||--------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|| Cold, Dry Morning | Raise charge temp 5-10 deg C, longer warm-up | Prevent hollow, underdeveloped cups || Hot, Humid Afternoon | Lower preheat, reduce early gas slightly | Prevent sharp spikes, over-roast || High Humidity | Extend drying and Maillard slightly | Avoid sourness, ensure full sweetness || Low Humidity | Monitor for tipping, reduce flame 5-10% | Maintain clarity without harshness |

D. Warm-Up Routine (Clarified)

  • Run dry for 15-30 minutes before first roast.- Use room thermometers or digital sensors.- Pre-warm green beans inside the roast room for 2+ hours.Why?- Stabilizes metal = steadier RoR.- Warmer green = less thermal shock.- Prevents early flat curves or excessive lag.

E. Roast Environment Strategy Table

| Situation | Adjustment | Flavor Impact ||------------------------|--------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|| Beans feel cold | Raise charge temp 5-10 deg C | Prevent flattening in early RoR || Rainy day | Slightly extend drying + Maillard | Avoid tipping, allow sweetness dev. || Hot summer afternoon | Reduce preheat and early gas | Preserve acidity and avoid roast drift|| Overnight cool metal | Increase warm-up time | Steady curve onset, fewer defects || RoR flattening post-crack | Raise airflow 10-15% or reduce gas slightly | Restore dev control, reduce harshness |

F. Analogy - Campfire Cooking

  • On windy cold days, soup takes longer to boil.- On humid days, drying onions takes longer.- Roasting is the same: ambient shifts = real heat impact.

G. Real-World Practice

I. Sustainability Note

  • Adjusting for environment = fewer ruined batches.- Reduces unnecessary waste, re-roasting, and bean loss.- Shows respect for origin by preserving flavor potential.

J. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: "My roast ran 1 minute longer than usual on a foggy day. What could I have done?"**Answer**: High humidity slowed drying. Try raising charge temp or improving airflow next time. Log weather to compare future batches.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

Lesson 1.1 – What Changes During Coffee Roasting?

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will clearly understand how roasting transforms green coffee into drinkable coffee by triggering physical, chemical, and sensory changes. You will start seeing roasting not as a recipe, but as a controlled process of transformation.

New Terms and Concepts (Introduced Before the Lesson)

TermMeaning
Green CoffeeThe raw, unroasted seeds of the coffee cherry. They are hard, dense, and smell grassy, not like coffee yet.
RoastingApplying heat to green coffee beans to trigger chemical and physical changes, making them brown, aromatic, grindable, and brewable.
Maillard ReactionA chemical reaction between sugars and amino acids that causes browning and complex flavor formation (also happens when baking bread or searing meat).
CaramelizationThe breaking down of sugars under heat, creating sweet, rich flavors and aromas.
First CrackThe first audible cracking sound beans make as internal pressure builds up and moisture/gas escapes — signals the beginning of development.
Second CrackA softer, more rapid cracking that happens later if roasting continues — marks deep structural breakdown and very dark roast territory.
DensityHow tightly packed the bean’s material is. High density = harder bean, low density = softer bean. Roasting lowers bean density.
CO₂ FormationThe generation of carbon dioxide gas inside the bean during roasting. Important for crema (foam) in espresso and freshness in brewed coffee.
SolubilityHow easily substances in the coffee dissolve into water during brewing. Roasting increases solubility by breaking down bean structure.

Main Lesson Content

A. Why Do We Roast Coffee?

In its green, raw form, coffee is:

Undrinkable — hard, grassy, acidic.

Not flavorful — lacks the rich smells and tastes we associate with brewed coffee.

Roasting solves this by:

Creating flavors through the Maillard reaction and caramelization.

Releasing aromas that are locked inside green beans.

Making coffee soluble, meaning water can pull out flavors easily.

Transforming bean structure — making them easier to grind and brew.

Without roasting, coffee would taste like boiled peas or grass!

B. Physical Changes During Roasting

ChangeWhat Happens
SizeBeans expand by 50–60% in volume.
ColorGreen → Yellow → Tan → Brown → Dark Brown.
WeightBeans lose about 12–18% of their weight due to water loss and gas release.
CracksBeans physically pop: First Crack (release of steam and gases); Second Crack (bean structure breakdown if roasted further).

C. Chemical Changes During Roasting

ReactionWhat It Means
Moisture LossBeans lose water (first 3–6 minutes), necessary for further chemical reactions.
Maillard ReactionSugars and amino acids react to create new flavor compounds, starting around 140–160°C.
CaramelizationSugars break down further around 170°C, adding sweetness, aroma, and body.
CO₂ FormationChemical breakdown releases trapped gases, creating crema and influencing freshness.

D. Sensory Outcomes — How the Bean’s Taste Changes

Roast StageFlavorAcidityBodySweetnessBitterness
LightBright, fruityHighLightLow–MediumLow
MediumBalanced, sweetMediumMedium–FullMedium–HighLow–Medium
DarkChocolatey, smokyLowFullLow–MediumHigh

In short:

Light roasts = bright, fruity, higher acidity.

Medium roasts = balanced sweetness, fuller body.

Dark roasts = heavier, smoky, more bitterness.

Key Takeaways

Roasting is transformation. It’s not magic — it’s a controlled sequence of physical and chemical changes.

Every stage matters. How you dry, brown, and develop beans affects flavor, acidity, body, and sweetness.

Even before roasting yourself, learning to watch, smell, and listen during a roast builds real intuition.

Optional Action Step

If you can watch a roast (live or on YouTube):

Make a 3-column log:

Time | Visible Change | Smell/Sound.

Note when:

Yellowing occurs (green turns pale).

First crack happens (light popping sounds).

Drop/Stop occurs (roast ends).

If you can’t observe a roast:

Buy two roasted coffees — one lighter, one darker.

Compare their:

Color

Smell

Feel

Taste (flavor, body, acidity, bitterness).

Reflection Prompt

What surprised you the most about how much coffee changes during roasting?

Why do you think the “first crack” is so important when controlling the roast?

End of Lesson 1.1

Self-study caution

The sequence matters more than memorizing exact numbers. Watch color, smell, sound, and texture first; use temperatures only after you understand what your own roaster is measuring.

Before you continue

  • You can explain why green coffee is not yet brewable.
  • You can name the main physical and chemical changes during roasting.
  • You can identify First Crack as a practical control point, not a magic switch.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

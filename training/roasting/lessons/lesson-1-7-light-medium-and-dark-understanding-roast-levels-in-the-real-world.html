Lesson 1.7 – Light, Medium, and Dark: Understanding Roast Levels in the Real World

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand the general definitions of roast levels (light, medium, dark),

Learn how roast level affects flavor, body, and aroma,

Recognize that visual, aromatic, and flavor cues are influenced by personal and environmental factors,

Learn how to navigate subjective differences wisely and build your own reliable skills.

New Terms and Concepts (Introduced Before Lesson)

TermMeaning
Roast LevelThe degree of roasting, often categorized as light, medium, or dark based on color, flavor, and development.
Agtron ScaleA standardized color measurement system for roasted coffee.
Surface Oil MigrationAppearance of oils on bean surface at darker roast levels.
Development Time Ratio (DTR)The percentage of total roast time spent after First Crack.
Origin CharacterThe unique natural flavors related to the bean’s variety, altitude, and processing method.

Main Lesson Content

A. Why Roast Level Matters

Roast level is one of the strongest influences on the final cup:

It shapes acidity, sweetness, bitterness, body, and aroma,

It alters bean structure (making it easier or harder to brew),

It influences consumer preference depending on region, culture, and brewing method.

Understanding roast level empowers you — whether roasting, buying, or brewing.

B. Broad Categories of Roast Levels (Guidelines Only)

Roast LevelVisual ClueFlavor FocusTexture
LightLight brown, dry surfaceBright acidity, florals, fruitLight body
MediumMedium brown, dry to slight sheenBalanced sweetness, chocolate, caramelSmooth, rounded
DarkDark brown/black, oily surfaceBitter-sweetness, smokiness, toastinessHeavy body

Important:

These are guides, not rigid laws.

Temperature, roasting style, bean type, and interpretation all influence outcomes.

C. Measuring Roast Level: Human Eyes and Machines

Visual Signs:

Surface color: Lighter = less roasted, Darker = more roasted.

Oil appearance: Oily sheen appears at darker levels.

Expansion: Dark roasts usually puff more due to structural breakdown.

Machine Tools (optional, not mandatory):

ToolTypical Usage
Agtron ScaleStandard lab-grade roast color scoring (Light = 65–80+, Medium = 55–65, Dark = <45).
Lighttells CM-100Handheld color reader for field use.
ColorTrackIndustrial color analyzer for commercial roasters.

Cautions:

Machine readings vary slightly.

Room lighting, bean moisture, and surface oils can affect readings.

Human eyesight varies by age, experience, environment.

D. How Development Time and Flavor Connect to Roast Level

DTR (%)Likely Roast Level
15–20%Light
20–25%Medium
25–30%+Medium-Dark to Dark

Reminder:

Roast level is not just a color — it reflects the entire roast journey, including curve shape, airflow, and energy management.

F. Realities of Color Perception (Important!)

ChallengeImpact
Lighting (warm vs cool bulbs)Beans may appear darker or lighter than reality
Shadows and glareCan distort color reading
Human vision differencesColor perception varies with age, health, and practice

Therefore:

Learn to use multiple clues together — color + smell + structure + taste — not just color alone.

Build your own internal reference scale over time by comparing many coffees and practicing observation.

H. Practical Exercises (No Roaster Required)

Option 1 — Visual Bean Study:

Gather roasted coffees from different sources.

Arrange them by eye from lightest to darkest.

Smell the beans. Break some open and look inside.

Taste side by side if possible.

Option 2 — Explore Online or Sample Sets:

Use curated sample sets (e.g., Blue Tokai, Third Wave, Black Baza).

Read roast notes and compare with your own perceptions.

Tip:

Keep a simple notebook:

Bean type, origin

Visual impression

Smell notes

Brew notes (if you can taste)

Key Takeaways

Roast level influences flavor, body, and brewing behavior.

Color is a helpful clue, but not absolute — use smell, texture, and taste too.

Machines help but are not infallible.

Each roaster’s eye, equipment, and context differ — learn to adapt and calibrate yourself gently over time.

Reflection Prompt

If two coffees are called “light roast” but look quite different, what factors could explain it?

What matters more — the color, or how the coffee actually tastes to you?

End of Lesson 1.7

Self-study caution

DTR helps compare development after First Crack, but roast level should be judged with color, end temperature, sensory result, bean structure, and roast age.

Before you continue

  • You can describe light, medium, and dark roast as broad categories.
  • You know color is useful but not absolute.
  • You can avoid using DTR as the only roast-level measurement.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

Gas Management & Airflow Tuning

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Gas and airflow shape heat transfer, smoke removal, convective energy, and clarity. Air is not a background variable.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds machine-sensory connection.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Understand how airflow and gas control affect heat transfer and flavor development in roasting - and how to tune them effectively even without a roaster.

A. Why Gas and Airflow Matter

  • These are your main controls of heat delivery- Misuse = flat, smoky, or harsh coffee- Mastery = clean, sweet, consistent cups

B. What Each Control Does

| Control | What It Affects | Consequence of Misuse ||-------------|---------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|| Gas | Rate of Rise (RoR) - how fast temp climbs | Scorching, under/over development || Airflow | Heat evacuation, smoke removal | Smoky flavors, tipping, flavor loss || | Conduction vs convection balance | Unstable RoR, flavor taints |

C. Heat Transfer and Airflow Interplay

  • See infographic: Airflow and Heat Transfer by Roast Phase- Conduction = drum-to-bean heat (early phase)- Convection = hot air circulation (later phase)- Strategy: - Early roast: Low airflow, strong gas = conduction - Maillard: Medium airflow balances drying and sweetness - Post-crack: High airflow = clear cup, smoke removal

D. Tuning Gas Input by Roast Phase

| Phase | Gas Strategy | Troubleshooting Tip ||----------------|--------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|| Charge | Medium to high for thermal momentum | Too low = long drying, flattening || Drying | Maintain high enough to drive off moisture | Spiking? -> lower gas 10% post yellowing || Maillard | Reduce gas to slow RoR, promote sweetness | Too fast? -> step down gas by 10-15% || First Crack | Maintain moderate to avoid runaway RoR | Overshooting? -> reduce 5-10% pre-crack || Development | Adjust gas to stretch dev time, reduce harshness | Flat finish? -> maintain minimum steady input |

E. Tuning Airflow (with Origin Context)

See infographic: Phase-by-Phase Airflow Control| Phase | Airflow Strategy | Origin Consideration ||---------------|--------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|| Charge | Closed or low to build heat | Use low for all origins || Drying | Low-medium to release moisture | Wet-processed coffees benefit from early drying || Maillard | Medium - avoid tipping, preserve aromatics | Yirgacheffe (high-density): moderate to preserve florals || First Crack | High - clear smoke, prevent taints | Robusta (low-density): needs clean airflow || Development | Maintain high for clean finish | All origins benefit from clean-out phase |

F. Beginner Analogy: Stove Flame & Kitchen Vent

  • Gas = Burner flame -> Controls cooking speed- Airflow = Kitchen exhaust -> Prevents smoke buildup- Good airflow keeps flavors clean - bad airflow ruins aroma

G. Visual Cue Practice (No Roaster Needed)

I. Sustainability Note

  • Managing airflow well = cleaner cups with fewer re-roasts- Origin flavor is preserved, not masked by smoke- Good roasts = better scores = fairer prices for farmers

J. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: 'My coffee tasted harsh and dry despite hitting target time.'Answer: Possibly high gas during development or low airflow. Try reducing post-crack gas 10% and raising airflow.Prompt 2: 'My roast spiked in RoR after 1st crack.'Answer: Reduce gas by 5-10% just before crack. Watch curve flatten gently into development.

K. Glossary

  • Airflow: Fan/exhaust setting that affects convection and clarity- Gas: Heat input (flame or electric) that drives RoR- RoR: Rate of Rise ( deg C/min) tracking heat speed- Development: Time post-1st crack for flavor finishing- Smoke Contamination: Flavor taint from retained exhaust gases

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

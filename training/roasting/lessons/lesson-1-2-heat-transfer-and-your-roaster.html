Lesson 1.2 – Heat Transfer & Your Roaster

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will understand:

How heat moves into coffee beans during roasting,

How different roasters use conduction, convection, and radiation,

How this affects roast speed, bean quality, and flavor outcomes,

How to observe and manage heat flow during roasting.

New Terms and Concepts (Introduced Before Lesson)

TermMeaning
Heat TransferThe movement of heat energy from a hot object or environment into the coffee bean.
ConductionHeat transfer through direct contact (like a pan heating your hand if you touch it).
ConvectionHeat transfer through a moving fluid — in roasting, it means hot air moving around the beans.
RadiationHeat transferred through energy waves, like infrared heat from an oven or fireplace.
ScorchingBurn marks on the bean from excessive surface contact heating (usually a conduction problem).
TippingBurn marks at the tips or edges of beans due to uneven or too-fast heating.
BakingA flat, dull flavor defect caused by a roast slowing too much, often from insufficient heat during the middle phases.
Charge TemperatureThe starting temperature of the roaster drum or air when you load green beans.
Rate of Rise (RoR)How quickly the bean temperature increases, usually measured in degrees per minute.
Fluid Bed RoasterA type of roaster that moves beans around by blowing hot air through them, like a hairdryer.
Drum RoasterA roaster where beans are tumbled inside a rotating drum heated by gas or electricity.

Main Lesson Content

A. Why Heat Transfer Matters

When roasting coffee, you are not simply heating beans — you are managing how heat is delivered.

Too much heat too fast → Scorching (burn marks) or Tipping (burnt bean tips).

Too little heat → Baking (dull, lifeless flavor).

Uneven heat → Inconsistent roast color and taste.

Roast mastery = Heat flow mastery.

B. The Three Forms of Heat Transfer

TypeDescriptionCommon in These Roaster Types
ConductionHeat by direct contact. Beans touching a hot metal drum absorb heat.Traditional drum roasters
ConvectionHeat by moving hot air. Beans heat as air passes over them.Fluid bed roasters, modern drum roasters with airflow control
RadiationHeat from energy waves (infrared or coil elements).Some gas or electric hybrid roasters

Important:

Most real-world roasters use a blend of all three — but usually, one mode dominates depending on machine design.

C. How Roaster Design Affects Heat Transfer

Roaster FeatureHow It Affects Heat
Drum SpeedFaster spinning = more convection (air exposure); slower = more conduction (surface contact).
Airflow ControlHigher airflow = more convection; lower airflow = more conduction effect.
Charge TemperatureSets the starting momentum; higher charge = faster initial heating (more conduction).
Drum MaterialThicker drums absorb and release heat slower (steady heat); thinner drums react faster (can spike or crash easily).
Bean Load SizeLarger loads slow down heating (more mass to heat), affecting how fast and evenly beans roast.

D. Real-World Effects of Heat Transfer

If heat is too intense or badly balanced, it causes defects:

DefectCauseVisual/Taste Symptoms
ScorchingBean touches drum surface that’s too hot (excess conduction).Dark patches or burn marks on beans.
TippingOutside heats too fast, inside lags (uneven transfer).Small burns on bean tips or edges.
BakingRoast slows down too much during Maillard or development.Flat, dull, bready flavor — not burnt, but lifeless.

Good roasting is a steady dance:

Heat must keep moving into the bean.

No one form of heat (like conduction alone) should dominate too aggressively.

E. Your Roaster: Observing and Adjusting Heat Flow

If You Roast With…Watch For…You Can Adjust…
Sample Drum RoasterOver-scorching, small batch instability.Charge temp, drum speed, fan speed.
Fluid Bed Roaster (e.g., FreshRoast)Rapid heating, tipping risk.Lower heat settings early, control airflow.
Electric Drum RoasterSluggish response, low convection.Manage initial soak phase carefully, adjust power early.

Tip:

Roasters behave differently at different stages. Early in the roast, manage conduction and convection carefully to avoid runaway heat or stall.

Key Takeaways

Roasting = Managing heat movement, not just temperature numbers.

Balance conduction and convection for even roasting.

Observe your machine. Watch how fast the beans color, how evenly they heat, and how quickly they crack.

Your roast profile depends on your control of heat transfer, not just the beans or recipes.

Optional Practical Exercise

If you can roast a small batch:

Make 4 notes:

How fast the temperature rises (Rate of Rise).

Whether the color changes smoothly.

Whether you smell burning (early scorching) or notice excessive smoke.

When first crack begins and how it sounds (soft pops or violent snaps?).

Reflect:

Is the heating even?

Am I leading the roast, or chasing it?

If you cannot roast yet:

Watch roast videos online.

Try to spot when beans yellow, when first crack happens, and how smooth or uneven the roast looks.

Reflection Prompt

Why do you think managing heat type (conduction vs convection) matters more than just “following the thermometer”?

If a roast starts smelling smoky early, what might be happening inside the roaster?

Self-Check Quiz

Answer YES or NO:

Does conduction occur in air (fluid bed) roasters?

→ No

Can scorching happen in fluid bed roasters?

→ Yes

Is convection the dominant heat type in traditional drum roasters?

→ No (It’s mostly conduction unless airflow is strong.)

Is tipping caused by slow roasting?

→ No (It’s caused by too fast external heating.)

Can uneven airflow cause inconsistent roast color?

→ Yes

Scoring: 1 point per correct answer. No judgment — just self-awareness.

End of Lesson 1.2

Self-study caution

Heat transfer behaves differently in drum, fluid-bed, electric, and gas roasters. Treat the examples as principles, then calibrate them to your machine.

Before you continue

  • You can distinguish conduction, convection, and radiation.
  • You can describe why too much early contact heat can cause surface damage.
  • You can observe heat problems without blaming only one variable.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

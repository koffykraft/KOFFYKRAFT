Lesson 1.11 – Sample Roasting vs Production Roasting

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand the difference between sample roasting and production roasting,

Learn the purpose and goals of each,

Recognize how roast strategies differ depending on the situation,

Begin planning appropriate roast profiles based on the intended outcome.

1. What is Sample Roasting?

Sample roasting is roasting small batches of green coffee to evaluate the bean’s natural qualities.

Main goal: Reveal origin character (acidity, sweetness, defects, balance),

Kept clean, consistent, and neutral enough for bean judgment rather than optimized for one final drink style,

Common in green coffee buying, lot approvals, harvest monitoring.

2. What is Production Roasting?

Production roasting is roasting coffee to create a finished product for drinking.

Main goal: Craft a delicious, consistent cup for customers or personal use,

Roasts are adjusted to suit brewing methods, flavor preferences, and market needs,

Focus on drinkability, balance, sweetness, texture.

3. Key Differences Between Sample and Production Roasting

AspectSample RoastingProduction Roasting
PurposeBean evaluationBeverage creation
Batch SizeVery small (50g–500g)Medium to large (2kg–15kg+)
Roast TargetLight, neutral, revealingTargeted to desired flavor goal
End TemperatureLowerVariable (higher for darker profiles)
Curve ControlPriority on evenness, clarityPriority on body, sweetness, balance
EquipmentSample roasters (Ikawa, Probatino)Production roasters (Probat, Diedrich, Loring)

4. How Roast Profiles Differ

Roast PhaseSample Roast BehaviorProduction Roast Behavior
Drying PhaseModerate to quick (prevent bean damage)Controlled according to batch size and density
Maillard PhaseShort to moderate (clarity over body)Can be extended for body and flavor depth
Development PhaseShort (preserve acids, show defects)Tailored — short for brightness, long for bittersweetness

5. Why Not Use a Sample Profile for Production?

Sample roasts show what the bean can offer — but not how to maximize that in the cup.

Production roasts adjust:

Roast depth,

Maillard caramelization,

Development time,

Body building,

Bitterness control,

Different goals mean different curve shapes and decisions.

6. Practical Beginner Exercise

Option A — Compare Commercial Roasts:

Buy a light roast and a medium/dark roast from specialty cafés.

Observe:

Acidity level?

Body?

Sweetness?

Bitterness?

Guess: Was the roast closer to sample or production style?

Option B — Draft a Mini Roast Plan:

If asked to sample roast a new green coffee:

Charge Temperature: Medium (~190°C drum temp),

Fast but controlled Drying (3–4 min),

Shorter Maillard (preserve clarity),

Short Development (bright, clean finish).

7. Key Takeaways

Sample roasting reveals bean potential.

Production roasting shapes the final drinking experience.

Roast curves, phase timing, and goals differ between sample and production.

Skillful roasters adjust depending on purpose — not every roast is treated the same way.

Reflection Prompt

If you sample roast a bean and find it bright but thin,

how would you adjust your production roast to improve balance?

(Think about Maillard phase and Development time.)

End of Lesson 1.11

Self-study caution

Sample roasts should still be clean and drinkable. They are usually kept neutral and consistent so the green coffee can be judged fairly.

Before you continue

  • You can explain the purpose difference between sample and production roasting.
  • You know equipment examples are not requirements.
  • You can plan a sample roast for evaluation rather than a finished product style.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

Lesson 1.9 – Troubleshooting Roast Problems: Learning to See, Think, and Improve

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand how to observe, analyze, and respond to roasting issues,

Identify common roast defects confidently,

Learn a practical troubleshooting method,

Build a mindset of calm, skilled adaptation — not fear or blame.

Opening Note

All roasters, no matter how experienced, encounter roast defects.

They are signals, not failures.

Learning to read them is how a good roaster becomes a great one.

Roasting is part science, part listening, part adjusting.

Skill is not avoiding mistakes — it’s learning from them wisely.

1. Reading a Roast: Understanding the Curve First

Before troubleshooting, we must know what a healthy roast curve looks like.

Curve ShapeWhat HappensWhat It Tells You
Smooth Declining RoR (“Ski Slope”)Temperature rises steadily but slows gentlyEnergy is flowing correctly; roast is developing naturally
Flat RoR (“Plateau”)Temperature rise flattens too muchRisk of baking (dull, flat flavor)
RoR Flick (Small Bump After Crack)RoR rises again briefly after First CrackRisk of tipping, overdeveloped edges, harshness

Simple Guideline:

Smooth decline often suggests steady development.

Flat sections can suggest an energy stall and baking risk.

A flick can suggest late over-acceleration, but confirm with cup and bean appearance.

Roasting is not chasing a graph — it’s guiding energy carefully.

2. Recognizing Major Roast Defects

DefectVisual CluesFlavor CluesCommon Causes
UnderdevelopmentPale or uneven colorGrassy, sour, thinShort development phase, low RoR after Dry End
OverdevelopmentVery dark, oily beansHeavy bitterness, smokinessToo long after First Crack, high final temperature
BakingDull brown color, no glowFlat, papery, hollow cupFlat or stalled RoR during Maillard or Development
ScorchingBurnt patches, especially on bean flat sideSmoky, burnt edge tasteExcessive drum heat early, slow drum speed
TippingBurn marks on bean tips/edgesHarsh, astringent bitternessRoR spike after Turning Point
Cratering (Surface Blowouts)Small pits, ruptures on bean surfaceUsually minor flavor impact unless extremeRapid moisture expansion, uneven drying, too aggressive RoR
Flicks (RoR Flick After Crack)Curve bump visible; slight color unevennessSharp edge flavor, minor roughnessHeat surge after First Crack

3. Why Troubleshooting Is Not Always About You

Reality Factors That Influence Roasts:

Bean Density:

High-density beans resist heat more, need careful early energy.

Low-density beans absorb heat faster, tipping risk higher.

Moisture Content:

High-moisture beans need longer careful drying.

Old beans (low moisture) roast faster, higher scorching risk.

Machine Design:

Thin drum vs thick drum,

High vs low airflow,

Electric vs gas behavior.

Ambient Conditions:

Cold intake air can slow roast; hot weather can speed it up unexpectedly.

Good roasters respect the day's conditions and use recipes as starting points, not fixed commands.

4. Troubleshooting Flow: Calm, Logical, Clear

When a roast doesn’t taste right:

StepAction
1. Visual CheckPale? Burn marks? Craters? Oil?
2. Taste CheckSourness? Bitterness? Flatness? Sharpness?
3. Curve ReviewWas the RoR smooth? Flat? Spiking?
4. Roast Intent RecallWhat flavor did you aim for?
5. Bean/Environment CheckDensity, moisture, process, weather?

Then:

Decide calmly what was missing or excessive.

Plan a small, thoughtful adjustment next time.

5. Practical Exercise (Optional)

Option A (Without Roasting):

Watch a sample roasting video with a visible curve.

Pause at Dry End, First Crack, and Drop.

Sketch the curve mentally:

Was RoR smooth, flat, or flicking?

Option B (If Roasting):

After roasting, taste critically:

Are flavors hollow, sharp, heavy?

Compare curve behavior to cup results.

6. Final Encouragement

Every defect is a teacher.

Consistency comes from observation and thought, not chasing numbers.

Roasting is a conversation, not a command.

Patience. Curiosity. Skill.

They grow one roast at a time.

Reflection Prompt

If a roast showed surface cratering but tasted lively and sweet, would you call it a failure?

How could you roast differently if today’s beans were low-density naturals, compared to high-density washed coffees?

End of Lesson 1.9

Self-study caution

A RoR flick, flat spot, or crash is a signal to investigate. Probe noise, software smoothing, airflow, batch size, and bean behavior can all affect the curve.

Before you continue

  • You can troubleshoot with a sequence instead of guessing.
  • You can compare visual clues with cup clues.
  • You can make a small planned adjustment for the next roast.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

Production and Quality / Lesson 3.2

Sensory Calibration & Flavor Mapping

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Sensory calibration turns personal taste into useful roast feedback. Roasters must learn to describe before judging.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds sensory reliability.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Build the ability to identify, describe, and evaluate coffee flavors using calibrated sensory tools and repeatable reference practices.

A. Why Sensory Calibration Matters

  • It trains you to taste systematically.- Helps you communicate clearly with others.- Builds confidence and reduces subjectivity.

B. Calibration vs Cupping

| Type | Goal | Method ||----------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|| Calibration | Train perception | Use reference items, repetition, scoring || Cupping | Evaluate coffee | Use SCA protocol for roast/origin scoring |

C. Flavor References & Usage

| Reference | Purpose | How to Use ||----------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|| Lemon Juice | Citric acidity | Mix 10 ml with 50 ml water and taste || Apple Juice | Malic acidity | Taste, compare with washed Arabica || Jaggery Water | Fruity/brown sugar note | Mix 5g jaggery in 50 ml warm water || Cocoa Powder | Bitterness calibration | Taste raw powder and diluted version || Black Tea | Astringency | Brew strong and note drying mouthfeel |

D. Sensory Dimensions & Troubleshooting Tips

| Attribute | Examples | Calibration Tip ||---------------|--------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|| Aroma | Floral, nutty | Smell freshly ground spices/herbs || Acidity | Citric, malic | Taste lemon, apple, and compare to filter brew || Sweetness | Fruity, caramel | Compare jaggery water with plain water || Bitterness | Cocoa, overroast | Use cocoa or overbrewed tea to isolate || Body | Watery, silky, creamy | Compare cold brew (thin) vs espresso (thick) || Aftertaste | Lingering, clean, sharp | Note what remains 10 sec after swallowing |

F. Calibration Exercises (with Detail)

**Option 1: Reference Tasting at Home**1. Gather: lemon juice, jaggery water, cocoa powder, tea2. Prepare 50 ml of each liquid (with consistent dilution)3. Taste and rate: - Acidity (1-5) - Sweetness (1-5) - Bitterness (1-5) - Astringency (1-5)**Option 2: Paired Coffee Tasting**1. Buy 100g each of light and medium roast (e.g. from Blue Tokai)2. Brew 10g each using V60 at 1:16 ratio3. Blind taste and score: - Brightness - Body - Aftertaste4. Compare with your previous tasting notes or a friend's scores

G. Analogy - Learning a Flavor Language

  • Like learning a new language: first hard, later fluent.- The more you describe, the more you retain.- Flavor vocabulary = your tasting superpower.

H. Quiz (Self-Scoring)

1. Citric acid resembles: a) Vinegar b) Lemon juice c) Brown sugar2. Bitterness is most likely found in: a) Light roasts b) Robusta/dark roasts c) Washed coffees3. T/F: Acidity = sourness4. 'Balanced' usually refers to: a) High body b) Acidity and sweetness in harmony c) No roast defects5. Astringency feels like: a) Sweetness b) Clean finish c) Drying mouthfeel6. Paired tasting helps improve: a) Roast consistency b) Sensory comparison c) Bean moisture tracking**Answers**: 1=b, 2=b, 3=False, 4=b, 5=c, 6=b

I. Sustainability Note

  • Sensory calibration leads to better quality control.- Ensures origin-specific flavors are fairly valued.- Supports smallholder producers by reducing misjudgment of quality.

J. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: 'I always taste bitterness. What should I do?'**Answer**: Calibrate with cocoa powder and compare to your coffee. Then isolate sweetness using jaggery. Re-test with a balanced roast and track changes over time.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

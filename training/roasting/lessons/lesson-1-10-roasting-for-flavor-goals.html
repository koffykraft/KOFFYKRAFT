Lesson 1.10 – Roasting for Flavor Goals

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand how roast design affects the final flavor,

Identify the basic roast stages that influence different taste characteristics,

Plan roasting approaches based on clear flavor goals.

1. What Are Flavor Goals?

Flavor goals are the taste and texture outcomes you want to highlight in a coffee.

Common goals include:

Brightness (high acidity, lively flavor),

Sweetness (caramelization, balance),

Body (richness, thickness),

Bittersweetness (structured dark notes).

Choosing a flavor goal before roasting helps you manage heat and timing to suit the bean’s natural qualities.

2. Roast Phases and Flavor Impact

PhaseShorter/FasterLonger/Slower
Drying PhasePreserves acidity, livelinessSoftens acidity, smooths flavor
Maillard Phase (Browning)Lighter sweetness, floral/fruity notesDeeper sweetness, caramel, nuts
Development PhaseHighlights origin clarityLowers acidity, adds body and cocoa notes

Each phase influences flavor differently.

Adjusting the length and energy in each phase allows the roaster to guide the final taste.

3. Setting Flavor Goals and Basic Roast Plans

Flavor GoalRoast Plan Overview
BrightnessModerate charge, fast Drying, shorter Maillard, shorter Development
SweetnessModerate pace through Maillard, balanced Development (~20–24% of total time)
BodyHigher charge, longer Maillard, longer Development (>25% of total roast time)
BittersweetnessLonger Maillard and Development, higher drop temperatures

Note:

Higher DTR (Development Time Ratio) often increases body but can reduce brightness if extended too far.

4. Basic Adjustments to Influence Flavor

VariableEffect
Higher charge temperatureMore early momentum and faster drying; possible scorch/tip risk if mismanaged. Body still depends on the full roast plan.
Lower charge temperatureSlower start and potentially gentler early heat; may help control the roast but can risk sluggish development if taken too far.
Longer Maillard phaseMore caramelized sweetness and body
Shorter Maillard phaseHigher acidity, lighter body
Longer DevelopmentLower acidity, deeper body, risk of flatness if overextended
Shorter DevelopmentHigh clarity, but risk of underdeveloped flavors

5. Practical Beginner Example

Goal: Bright, clean coffee (e.g., washed Ethiopian for pour-over).

Plan:

Moderate charge (not too high),

Fast, but controlled Drying (~3–4 minutes),

Shorter Maillard (~3–4 minutes),

Short Development (~1.5–2 minutes after First Crack),

Drop before heavy caramelization (light-medium roast).

6. Key Takeaways

Flavor goals help structure roasting choices.

Each roast phase affects different flavor traits.

Heat management and timing adjustments are the basic tools for influencing the cup.

Planning with a goal reduces guesswork and improves consistency.

Reflection Prompt

If you want to highlight caramel sweetness and heavy body, which two phases should you extend slightly?

(Think about Maillard and Development.)

End of Lesson 1.10

Self-study caution

Charge temperature affects early momentum, but body and sweetness come from the full roast plan: drying, Maillard, development, airflow, final color, and cup result.

Before you continue

  • You can choose a flavor goal before planning a roast.
  • You can explain how each phase may influence flavor.
  • You can use DTR as one cue, not a guarantee.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.

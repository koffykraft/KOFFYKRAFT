Lesson 1.6 – Recognizing and Understanding Roast Defects

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Recognize the most common roast defects,

Understand the multiple causes behind them — not just the roaster’s actions,

Begin learning how to observe, analyze, and correct issues thoughtfully,

Understand that beans, machines, and environment all influence outcomes.

New Terms and Concepts (Introduced Before Lesson)

TermMeaning
Roast DefectAn unwanted outcome in flavor, aroma, or bean structure due to imbalanced roasting conditions.
UnderdevelopmentBean not roasted enough internally; tastes grassy or sour.
ScorchingSurface burn marks caused by excessive contact heat early in roast.
TippingSingeing at the edges/tips of the bean from sudden heat spikes.
BakingDull, lifeless flavor caused by roasting too slowly or without enough energy.
OverdevelopmentOverextending the roast after First Crack — leading to bitterness and heavy smokiness.
Thermal WaveHow energy moves through the bean — influenced by density, moisture, and heat application.

Main Lesson Content

A. What Are Roast Defects?

Roast defects happen when something disturbs the natural progression of chemical and physical changes inside the bean during roasting.

Defects are signals — not accusations.

They simply tell you that:

Heat entered too quickly or too slowly,

The bean couldn’t respond well (maybe due to density or moisture),

Timing between phases was mismatched.

Learning to read defects calmly is how you become a better roaster.

B. Common Roast Defects and Their Usual Causes

DefectLikely CauseTypical Flavor
UnderdevelopedToo short Development Phase; low heat energy; fast drying.Grassy, sour, hollow
ScorchingToo much direct heat early on; slow drum speed; poor airflow.Bitter spots, smoky tones
TippingSharp heat spike at/after turning point; sensitive beans.Singed edge bitterness
BakingRoasting too slowly through Maillard or Development phases; flat RoR.Lifeless, “paper,” dull
OverdevelopmentExcessive time after First Crack; high internal bean temp.Charred, smoky, heavy

D. Why Beans and Machines Matter Too

Roast defects are not always the roaster’s fault.

Factors outside your control that can still influence outcomes:

Low-density beans heat faster and tip easier.

High-moisture beans need longer drying and risk baking if rushed.

Old beans (low moisture) heat too fast and risk scorching.

Thick or thin drum materials transfer heat differently.

Small electric roasters may lag in heat changes, creating flat curves unintentionally.

Understanding context is as important as controlling technique.

F. Beginner-Friendly Root Cause Checklist

If a roast tastes off:

Look at the beans.

Pale? Burnt tips? Dull brown?

Think about the roast curve.

Did temperature rise too fast early?

Was the Rate of Rise steady or flat?

Recall bean properties.

High-density, low-moisture, or natural process?

Use multiple observations — not just one guess.

G. A Gentle Analogy: Cooking Pasta

ProblemPastaRoasting Parallel
UndercookedWhite core, tough biteUnderdeveloped bean
OvercookedMushy, broken strandsBaked or overdeveloped roast
ScorchedStuck to pan bottomScorching from surface heat

Just like pasta needs both good water temperature and careful timing, so does coffee.

H. Real-World Practice

Even if you don’t roast yet:

Watch two roasting videos:

One smooth and balanced.

One with “bad roast” issues.

Listen for First Crack timing, note Development Phase.

Observe bean surface at the end.

Guess: Was there underdevelopment, baking, or tipping?

Key Takeaways

Roast defects are signals, not punishments.

Bean characteristics, machine behavior, and heat management all interact to cause or avoid defects.

Calm observation is the first step toward better roasts.

Learning to see and think critically protects you from blame — and empowers growth.

Reflection Prompt

If a roast was baked but your curve looked smooth, what non-visible factors could have contributed?

How would you adjust roasting differently if using a low-density natural vs a high-density washed bean?

End of Lesson 1.6

Self-study caution

A defect usually has several possible causes. Confirm with visual clues, cup quality, roast log, machine behavior, and green coffee condition before changing the profile.

Before you continue

  • You can name common roast defects.
  • You can separate possible causes from confirmed causes.
  • You can make one small adjustment instead of changing everything at once.

Roast log prompt

Write one short note for each cue you can observe: time, color, aroma, sound, heat change, and cup result. If you cannot roast yet, use the same format while watching a roasting video or tasting two commercial coffees side by side.
